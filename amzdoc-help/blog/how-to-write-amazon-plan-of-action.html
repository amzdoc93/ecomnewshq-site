How to Write an Amazon Plan of Action That Actually Works (2026 Guide)

Your Amazon seller account is suspended. Amazon's email says you need to submit a "Plan of Action." You Google it, find a dozen templates, cobble something together, and submit. Amazon rejects it. Now you're worse off than before.

This guide is different. Based on 6+ years of writing Plans of Action that get accounts reinstated, we'll show you exactly what Amazon's Seller Performance team looks for — and what causes instant rejection.

What Is an Amazon Plan of Action?

A Plan of Action (POA) is the formal appeal document you submit to Amazon when your account or listing is suspended. It's your opportunity to demonstrate three things:

  1. Root Cause: You understand exactly what went wrong and why Amazon took action
  2. Corrective Actions: You've already taken specific steps to fix the immediate problem
  3. Preventive Measures: You've implemented systems to ensure it never happens again

Amazon's Seller Performance team reviews thousands of POAs weekly. They spend an average of 2-3 minutes on each one. Your POA needs to communicate everything they need in that window.

The 3-Part Structure Every POA Must Follow

Part 1: Root Cause Analysis

This is where most sellers fail. Amazon wants to see that you genuinely understand what triggered the suspension — not that you're making excuses or blaming others.

What Amazon wants:

  • Specific identification of the problem (which ASINs, which policy, which metrics)
  • Honest acknowledgment of what went wrong
  • Evidence that you've investigated the root cause, not just the symptoms
Common mistake: "We believe this was a misunderstanding" or "Customers may have received the wrong product by accident." These vague, deflecting statements signal to Amazon that you haven't truly identified the problem. Be specific and take ownership.

Good Root Cause Example:

  • Between January-March 2026, we received 4 inauthentic complaints on ASINs B07XXXXX and B08XXXXX.
  • Investigation revealed these products were sourced from Distributor XYZ, who we later discovered was not an authorized distributor for Brand ABC.
  • Our supplier vetting process failed to verify authorization status before placing orders.

Part 2: Corrective Actions (What You've Already Done)

This section describes the immediate steps you've taken to fix the problem. These must be actions already completed, not promises for the future.

What Amazon wants:

  • Specific, concrete actions (not vague commitments)
  • Actions that directly address the root cause you identified
  • Evidence that you've already implemented these changes

Good Corrective Actions Example:

  • Removed all inventory sourced from Distributor XYZ from FBA (removal order #XXXXX created March 15, 2026).
  • Deleted all listings for ASINs B07XXXXX and B08XXXXX that were sourced from unauthorized channels.
  • Contacted Brand ABC directly and obtained a letter confirming Distributor XYZ is not an authorized distributor.
  • Established a new supply relationship with Authorized Distributor DEF (Invoice #12345, dated March 20, 2026).

Part 3: Preventive Measures (How You'll Prevent Recurrence)

This is where you show Amazon you've built systems to prevent the same issue from happening again. These should be ongoing processes, not one-time fixes.

Good Preventive Measures Example:

  • Implemented a supplier verification checklist: all new suppliers must provide proof of authorization before any purchase order is placed.
  • Created a monthly audit process to review all active suppliers' authorization status.
  • Established a quality inspection protocol: all incoming inventory is checked against brand standards before shipment to FBA.
  • Trained all purchasing staff on Amazon's anti-counterfeiting policies and proper invoice documentation requirements.

POA Formatting Rules

How you format your POA matters almost as much as what you write. Amazon reviewers scan quickly, so make it easy for them:

  • Use bullet points — not paragraphs. Each point should be one clear action or finding.
  • Keep it 300-600 words — concise and specific beats long and vague.
  • Use clear headers — "Root Cause," "Corrective Actions," "Preventive Measures"
  • Include specific details — ASIN numbers, dates, supplier names, invoice numbers
  • No emotional language — "Please, this is my livelihood" works against you
  • No blaming Amazon — even if you think the suspension was unfair
Pro tip: Before submitting, read your POA from the perspective of an Amazon reviewer who handles 100+ appeals per day. Does every sentence serve a purpose? Can each point be understood in 5 seconds? If not, revise.

5 Mistakes That Get POAs Rejected

  1. Using template language. Amazon's reviewers see the same templates constantly. Phrases like "We take product authenticity very seriously" are red flags for a templated response.
  2. Being vague about actions. "We will improve our quality control" means nothing. Specify exactly what you changed, when, and how.
  3. Submitting without proper invoices. If Amazon requests invoices, they must be from authorized suppliers, within 365 days, and show quantities matching your sales.
  4. Submitting too quickly. A rushed POA submitted 2 hours after suspension is almost always weak. Take 24-48 hours to properly investigate and document.
  5. Submitting multiple weak appeals. Each rejection makes the next attempt harder. Amazon may route your case to a senior review team or issue a final decision after 2-3 failed attempts.

When to Get Professional Help

Consider professional help if:

  • Your account generates $10,000+ per month (the cost of professional help is a fraction of lost revenue)
  • You've already submitted one or more rejected appeals
  • Your suspension involves Section 3, counterfeit, or IP issues (these are significantly more complex)
  • You're unsure about the root cause of your suspension
  • Amazon has requested documentation you don't have
